WHAT THIS IS Yo's Library is a space that contains every possible page of text, and now every possible image, without storing any of them. Nothing is saved anywhere. Every page is computed on the spot from its address — and for text, that address is not a lookup key or a hash, it's the page's own 3,200 characters read as one giant number. Converting text into that number and converting the number back into text are exact opposites of each other, so the same address always decodes to the exact same page, whether you got there by BROWSE or by SEARCH. TEXT Each page holds 3,200 characters drawn from a 29-symbol alphabet (a-z, space, comma, period). SEARCH takes whatever you type, pads it out to a full page, and converts that page into its address. BROWSE does the reverse: paste any address and it's decoded straight back into the 3,200 characters it represents. Because addresses carry the full weight of that information, they end up thousands of characters long — there's no way to compress a page down to something short without losing the page. IMAGES BROWSE generates a 200x200 grid of random grayscale pixels, seeded the same way as text pages. UPLOAD lets you drop in a real photo, which gets hashed into its own address and displayed at full resolution and color. WHY IT'S ALL NOISE Almost everything you'll ever open here is gibberish — meaningless text, static-like images. That's not a bug, it's the entire point of the original concept. A page of English prose is one incredibly rare outcome among 29^3200 possible pages. That number has roughly 4,677 digits. For comparison: the number of atoms in the entire observable universe is only about 10^80 — a mere 81 digits. The space of possible pages in this library dwarfs the atoms in the universe so completely that the comparison barely means anything. You could search forever and almost certainly never see two identical pages of readable text. The same logic applies to images. A 200x200 grayscale image has 256^40000 possible pixel arrangements. Real, recognizable pictures — faces, landscapes, anything with edges and structure — occupy a practically invisible sliver of that space. You could generate images continuously since the beginning of time and never once land on something that looks like a photograph. Pure noise is not just the likely outcome here — it is, for all practical purposes, the only possible outcome. Everything runs client-side in your browser. Nothing you type or upload is ever sent anywhere.
